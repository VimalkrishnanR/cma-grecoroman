For the Benefit of all the People Forever

Greek and Roman Art Collection at the Cleveland Museum of Art

By Vimal Krishnan R

June 29th, 2026

In 1916, the Cleveland Museum of Art (CMA) held its inaugural exhibition. This exhibition's catalogue gives us a glimpse into how Clevelanders nurtured a fledgling museum. The catalogue describes the inaugural exhibition as one 'which the people of Cleveland have been looking forward for about a decade.' The museum's design and construction was led by 'Cleveland men.' And 'Cleveland art lovers' extended their earnest support for the museum's opening. The impressive museum is located at the sprawling Wade park, on land that was gifted by Jeptha Wade. In the 1892 deed of gift for the land, it is mentioned 'for the benefit of all the people forever.' It is this spirit of inclusion—since its inception in Cleveland—that has guided the CMA's evolution for over a century.

On its website, CMA proclaims its noble vision: 'to bring the pleasure and meaning of art to the broadest possible audience.'A major portion of the collections, comprising more than 66,500 artworks spanning 6000 years, are showcased on CMA's website for free. It offers free daily general admission. It is an open access institution that encourages the public to download, share, and creatively use images of public-domain artworks, all without permissions. What's more, its open access policy enables new data-driven interpretations of its archives and collections.

The CMA has indeed become—as described by the Washington Post—a 'cultural powerhouse' known for advancing digital innovations in the cultural sphere. Yet, the CMA's century-old Greek Revival building and the Greco-Roman motifs in its inaugural exhibition posters remind us of CMA's initial identity that may have been shaped by a classical aesthetic. On its 110th anniversary, this brief data-visual exploration of the current Greek and Roman art collections is a testament to the enduring presence of the classical aesthetic that defined the CMA's formative identity.

The Greek and Roman Art Department

In his ekphrastic poem, Ode on a Grecian Urn, John Keats captures the timeless quality of a Grecian urn. According to Keats, such classical artworks are our 'friends'.'They are 'witnesses' of our daily 'woes' and their sublime beauty directs us to a transcendental truth. The CMA's Greek and Roman Art Department is a treasure trove of such timeless artworks. As we see the several Red-Figure and Black-Figure ceramics, the 'Torso of a Kouros', the many dancing satyrs and marble portraits of emperors and empresses, we cannot help but remember Keats' lines:

      What men or gods are these? What maidens loth?
What mad pursuit? What struggle to escape?
              What pipes and timbrels? What wild ecstasy?

Greco-Roman Legacy: The 1916 Exhibition

Ralph King, a Cleveland real estate investor who was an early supporter of the museum, had a special interest in encouraging young artists. He was also a trustee of the Cleveland school of the arts. As the CMA was being constructed, King offered a $100 prize for the best poster that would be used to announce the museum's opening. Robert Roessler, a student at the school, won the prize. In his oil-on-paper poster, a stylised Parthenon looms large over CMA's Greek Revival building. The posters made by the other students, all with dominant Greco-Roman motifs, equally impressed King and he offered a second and a third prize. While there is no evidence that for the inaugural exhibition these posters served as promotional material, they are traces of how Clevelanders might have perceived CMA—a committed preserver of heritage rooted in a classical ethos. The exhibition catalogue begins with a description of 'Classic Art' and a list of exhibits that were showcased in the Rotunda. Though the catalogue lists 132 exhibits, some of them lent to the museum, currently the CMA holds 47 of these artworks. As shown in the pictogram above, while sculptures are dominant in the present collection, the inaugural exhibition showcased a greater proportion of ceramics. Nowadays, with just a flick of the finger, we can behold a terracotta Aphrodite or a silver stater bearing the Head of Athena. Thanks to the CMA's impressive virtual presence. Clevelanders have been vindicated. The fine artworks in their museum can now be seen by 'all the people' from everywhere in the world. The CMA is truly our ever-renewing 'friend' in the 'midst' of our 'woes.'

Methodology: The Cleveland Museum of Art offers information on its artwork collections through an open access api. I filtered the json data by specifying 'Greek and Roman Art' in the 'Department' parameter. Using python and pandas, I converted the json data into a pandas data frame for further analysis. For historical context, I referred to copies of the inaugural exhibition catalogues available on the website of Smithsonian Libraries. The data visualisations and table have been made using Flourish.
